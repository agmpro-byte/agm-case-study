The Client

A pest control operator in California's Central Valley: roughly 2,500 active customers, a recurring book north of $2M annually, and their entire operation — routing, scheduling, billing — running on one of the industry's leading practice management platforms. The platform is genuinely good at that job, and nothing in this case study changes that.

AGM Pro Tools runs their operations intelligence layer: a dashboard built on a three-times-daily sync that turns raw operational data into prioritized queues — who to collect from, who's about to churn, where the cross-sell whitespace is.

The Question

Looking at the scoreboard — monthly recurring revenue, active customers, cancellation rate, autopay coverage — the owner asked the most natural question in business:

"Can you show me how these numbers compare to last year?"

Every owner assumes that answer lives in their software somewhere. It's their business. Their numbers. Surely the system that processes every invoice and every route knows what the business looked like last June.

It doesn't. And discovering exactly why it doesn't turned into the most important finding of the engagement.

The Discovery

When we went digging for the historical values to build the comparison, we found a structural truth about operational software — not a bug, not a missing feature, but a design fact that holds across the field service industry:

Operational platforms store today. They do not store history.

  • Autopay enrollment — overwritten in place. What it was last month is gone.
  • Account balances and aging — current values only. Yesterday's balance no longer exists anywhere.
  • Customer status — more than 4,000 of the operator's ~6,900 inactive records carried no date for when the customer actually left.

The platform's job is to answer "what needs to happen today?" — and it answers brilliantly. But that means "what was my MRR last June?" isn't hidden or hard to reach. It is nonexistent. No report, no export, no API call can produce a number the system never wrote down.

Every month that passes without someone recording the trend line is a month of business history erased — permanently.

The 90-Day Reconstruction

One partial exception: recent cancellation records still held their dates. That meant the last 90 days could be rebuilt — carefully — from raw account and subscription data.

We validated the method the honest way: we reconstructed today's numbers using only the raw records, then compared them against the live computed KPIs. They matched to the penny. Same formulas, same classification rules, provably correct.

That let us ship month-over-month deltas to the operator's scoreboard the same day the question was asked — every core metric now carrying a plus-or-minus against 30 days prior, color-coded by whether the movement is good news or bad. Recurring revenue: up. Active customers: down a few. Cancellation rate: a hair above the same window last month. Real answers, same day.

But 90 days was the hard ceiling. Reconstruction could buy a month of hindsight. It could never buy a year.

What We Built

So we changed what our platform does with the data it already syncs. Once per day, the sync now banks a permanent point-in-time record of the business — nine core numbers, written down and never overwritten:

Monthly recurring revenue · annualized book · active customers · active service plans · net new plans · cancellation rate · autopay coverage · route density · completion rate

The part that matters most: it's structural, not manual

The history capture lives inside the sync engine itself — not in a dashboard, not in a scheduled report someone has to maintain. Nobody triggers it. Nobody remembers it. And every new client we onboard inherits it automatically from their very first sync: the longitudinal record starts on day one, with zero extra setup.

Thirty days in, every metric gets an honest month-over-month comparison from stored truth instead of reconstruction. A year in, true year-over-year — the exact question that started all this — answers itself. Forever.

Why It Compounds

Here is the part worth sitting with: this data cannot be backfilled. If the record starts in July, it starts in July — there is no catching up, no buying the missing months, no engineering that recovers a number nobody wrote down.

For the operator, that record becomes an asset that grows in value every single day it runs: real seasonality curves, early warning when autopay coverage starts slipping, the difference between "this summer feels better" and "this summer is 6% better." For an owner who ever sells the business, it's the operational history a buyer's diligence team wishes every target had.

Trend intelligence isn't a report you run. It's a discipline something has to be doing every single day — and now, something is.

Results
